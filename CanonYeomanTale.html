Summary

The Canon’s Yeoman is a servant of an alchemist. The Canon and his Yeoman overtake the rest of the travelling pilgrims, as they had left later than the other pilgrims. As the Host implores the Canon’s Yeoman to tell of the craft that he and his master practise, the Canon rides off, ashamed of what the Yeoman may say. In the first part of his tale, the Canon’s Yeoman describes the craft of alchemy and warns the listeners to not pursue the craft, as it will bring any who practise it to ruin. The Canon’s Yeoman also tells of the Alchemists Stone, which he and his master have been trying to discover. In the second part of his tale he describes how a fiendish canon, who he states was not his master, deceived a good-willed priest and robbed him of his money.

Title Explanation

The title, The Canon’s Yeoman’s tale, is quite self-explanatory. The Canon’s Yeoman is telling a tale.

Message

I believe that the message of this tale is that you must be honest, and that you should not deceive people for selfish desires.

Topic

The topic of this tale is the shady business that canons and alchemists practised in Chaucer’s time.

Tone

The tale has a negative tone and seems to be a critique on alchemists and canons.

Perspective

This poem is written from the perspective of the Yeoman, however, for the second part of the poem, the Yeoman acts as an omniscient narrator.

Rhyme Scheme

The tale is written in Iambic Pentameter, with an aa/bb/cc/dd etc. rhyme scheme.

Background of this poem

The tale reflects Chaucer’s opinion on alchemists. In Chaucer’s time there was a man named William de Brumley who admitted to falsifying gold coins as he was taught by an alchemist named William Shuchirch, who worked at King’s Chapel, Windsor, where Chaucer had supervised repairs in 1390. It is believed that Chaucer may have based his tale on these men.
